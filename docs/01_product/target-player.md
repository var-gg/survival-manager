# Target Player

- Status: draft
- Last Updated: 2026-03-29
- Phase: concept framing

## Purpose

This document defines the initial target-player hypothesis for `survival-manager`.
This is a framing tool for product decisions, not a final market segmentation claim.

## Primary Target Player Hypothesis

The likely primary player is someone who:

- enjoys management-heavy or setup-heavy strategy games
- likes watching a prepared plan execute in combat
- values formation, unit priority, composition, and build planning more than direct action control
- enjoys repeated adjustment after observing success or failure
- plays on PC and is comfortable learning layered systems over repeated runs

## Secondary Target Player Hypothesis

A secondary audience may include players who:

- enjoy auto-battle or auto-resolve flavored strategy experiences
- enjoy deckbuilding and combinational decision making
- like observer-style play where preparation matters more than reflex
- want replayability and progression without requiring constant high-speed input

## Likely Fit Signals

Potential players may respond well if they want:

- the satisfaction of "my setup worked"
- tactical management without heavy micromanagement during combat
- short-to-medium session runs
- visible build identity shifts between runs
- a sense of compounding strategic mastery over time

## Likely Friction Signals

Potential players may bounce if they want:

- direct action combat as the main source of skill expression
- classic turn-based SRPG pacing with explicit stop-and-command flow
- pure autobattler economy loops with minimal adventure/progression framing
- extremely light systems with almost no management overhead

## Market Framing Constraint

The target player profile should stay narrow enough that Steam positioning remains understandable.
If the game tries to equally serve turn-based tactics players, action-roguelite players, deckbuilder players, and deep management players at full depth, it may become hard to position and harder to finish.

## Early Audience Risk

The current concept may attract curiosity from multiple niches while fully satisfying none.
This is a discovery risk that should be tested with prototypes, not assumed away.

## Open Questions

- Is the primary audience closer to tactical auto-battle players or build-crafting roguelite players?
- How much management overhead is satisfying before it becomes exhausting?
- How much direct intervention does the target player actually want during combat?
- What feature would make the right player immediately say, "this is for me"?
